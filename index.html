Your own engineering and services — what we build and stand behind, not what the community gave.

Drivers and community work live in the open core, where everyone shares them. We monetize assurance, management, and support — the things that are genuinely ours to sell. If we ever take a community contribution into a commercial component, it's under a clear, up-front agreement, never a surprise.

Where your money goes

A different kind of vendor: this isn't a margin business. It's a way to keep the work funded.

LoricaOS is not built to maximize profit. Every dollar that comes in through the commercial edition is pointed back at the thing itself:

  • Independent security audits — paying outside experts to attack the kernel and find what we can't.
  • Paid engineering — funding real driver, kernel, and userspace work, all of it landing in the open core that everyone uses.
  • Keeping it alive — the goal is to break even and stay funded, not to extract.

That's not just principle — it's why the incentives are safe to trust. There's no investor pressure to rug-pull a license, no reason to starve the open core to upsell you. When you pay for LoricaOS Enterprise, you're funding a more-audited, better-supported, more-secure system that stays open. The money compounds into the commons, not away from it.

What the enterprise edition is for

One primitive — capability-confined isolation — applied to the three things organizations most need to keep apart.

Isolate workloads building

A minimal, attestable, capability-confined type-1 hypervisor. Run untrusted tenants — or a rented-GPU AI workload — with a trusted base small enough to audit, instead of a 30-million-line host you have to take on faith.

Isolate secrets building

The OS that guards the keys, not the one that runs the compute. Encrypted-at-rest, capability-gated, attested — the root of trust an AI or data deployment asks before it releases anything.

Isolate authority building

Warden — a capability-native directory. Fleet identity and policy expressed as capability grants, not registry sprawl. Software-airgap a role by simply not granting it the network.

Support & SLA

Direct access to the people who wrote it, and a tested stable channel rather than the bleeding edge.

Certified builds

Reproducible, signed, attestable images on a validated hardware list — so "it's running trusted LoricaOS" is something you can verify.

Long-term assurance

The audits and hardening your subscription funds, delivered back as a system you can defend to a regulator.

Who it's for

Not "a lighter Linux for everyone." A sharper tool for the people who have to prove isolation, not just claim it.
